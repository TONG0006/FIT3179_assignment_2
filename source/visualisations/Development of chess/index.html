Introduction

Chess, Western Chess or international chess is a well known board game played between two players. This infographic aims to take you through the chess development stage and where it stands today.

Many of the text references technical chess terms, however you don't need to fully know how to play chess to read this (but if you want to know the basic rules or learn, you can find the link by pressing the hamburger button located on the top right of the webpage).

This webpage makes use of the chessbase API and allows interactivy of chess moves. On the right is the puzzles API, feel free to use it. Once you are done with the fun, lets take a journey through the history of chess.

Chaturanga - 6th century

Before we talk about chess, we have to talk about its predeccesor, "Chaturanga"; in fact, Chaturanga is also the motivation of many other board games such as Japanese chess (Shogi), Chinese chess (Xiangqi), modern Indian chess and lots more.

Originating from ancient India in the 6th century, it had a slightly different setup to what we know as "Modern Western chess". Shown on the right, the black and white Queen and King were originally were not opposing each other.

Besides the visual appearance and setup by Chaturanga, the piece movements, win conditions or rules is unclear and left only to speculations.

Romantic chess - 18th century

The era is set between 1700s-1872s, players were exploring ideas on quick and tactical maneuvers of pieces. The moves in this era can be described as aggresive and playful.

A characterization of the openings in the era consisted of 1.e4 with the most played follow up being ..e5 2.Nf3 or ..e5 2.f4. The Sunburst chart categorises the first few moves into layers shown to the right, where the first move starts from the inner ring and branches out for the following moves.

The era was thought to be at its peak with their leaders of the era's style Alexander McDonnell and Louis-Charles Mahé de La Bourdonnais.

The era was famous for "The Immortal Game" which was fabricated on 21st of June 1851 entitled as a "supreme example of romantic chess". The scene was set in London where Karl Ernst Adolf Anderssen played a beautiful sequence of piece sacrificies to deliver a finisher onto Lionel Kieseritzky. Here is the game annotated by Thomas.

Just like the game above, where it emphasizes playing in style and expressing emotional expression, the Romantic era in other fields such as arts was just the same.

Due to this, top players such as Paul Morphy and the general public often shunned positional play as "dull", "cowardly" or "stingy". Despite their preference of flashy and dashing moves, positional, theoretical and closed games weren't completely unknown featuring in a few tournaments such as in London.

However, this would all change dramatically with the next few eras of chess, with the first biggest influencer being Wilhelm Steinitz.

Scientific Chess - 19th century

During a Vienna 1873 chess tournament, Wilhelm Seinitz demonstrated his form of positional play that utilises key concepts such as such as controlling the center with pawns, playing a closed position. The tournament consisted of 12 players strong players from 6 different countries and he went ahead and took first place after defeating Joseph Henry Blackburne in the finals.

His deployment of his theories for positional play had influenced many players and at the time and future players from his ideas. Wilhelm was regarded as one of the most influential chess theoritician and had driven the community into the scientific era.

While there were still people who thought his playstyle was cowardly, Wilhelm also showed that over the long term, the setup can lead to more ferocious attacks than the last generation. After the tournament, he was considered to be the strongest player in the world and when the first official World Chess Championship was held in 1886, he took the title of being the first official World Chess Champion.

Here is an example of a very instructive game by Steinitz himself a year before he claimed his title in 9th of Februrary 1885, playing for center control, space in a closed position and using tactical knight maneuvers to exploit the oppositions cramped space. Hypermodern chess - 20th century

Fast forward a few decades, chess has gone through multiple major theoretical advancements in tactical play. Once again another figure Aron Nimzowitsch, who is now known as the founder and leading practionier of hypermodernism had challenged some of the ideas that Steinitz had proposed.

He had also published a book "Mein System" or "My System" introducing many new and important concepts that are the backbone of todays theory.

It had introduced and formalised concepts such as pawn chain, overprotection, undermining, prophylaxis, restraint, rook on the seventh rank, knight outposts, the dynamics of the isolated queen's pawn, and other areas of chess which are covered in three different parts "The Elements", "Positional Play", and "Illustrative Games".

One of the main concepts that Hypermodernism rejected from Scientific chess was the idea to control the center with pieces instead of pawns.

While the occupying the center with pawns lead to a great control over the board and bigger space, Nimzowitsch had demonstrated that they can be a liability, becoming a target for the opponent.

The use of pieces controlling the center had an indirect control allowing an attack towards the enemies pawns and also allows the piece to be placed on central squares when possible.

While there are a few rejection against the Scientific Era, it is important to note that unlike Scientific Era to Romantic era, The Hypermodern framework acts as an extension to classical theory.

Additionally, from the hypermodern framework, these well established openings have been created. While most of these have only one standard lines to get to the position, most of these openings can be obtained through transpositions and hence the FEN was given instead. From here on, chess becomes more internationalised and unified as a sport. In 1924, Fédération Internationale des Échecs, FIDE more commonly known as International Chess Federation was founded.

In 1946, FIDE took over the administration of the World Championship and had continued to make the event a regular one over the years.

Dynamic chess - 21st century

We are now catching up with the current times and yet another era has emerged from this. The dynamism era by many different famous players and is currently still an active area of research and has gone through some breakthroughs and rejection. There has been many major and important events leading up to this era but will be too long to cover in here, hence I will be talking about the breakthroughs in what the 21st century has given us, computers.

It is the year 1997, the final round of a 6-match rematch from 1996 against Garry Kasparov the number one chess player at the time. A machine named Deep Blue were at a score of 2.5-2.5 against him.

This marked one of the first historical moment in chess where a machine had beaten a player. This had sparked many viewers at the time and also chess players as well, which motivated the idea of what we now refer to as chess engines.

The matched had attracted such attention that it had inspired many documentaries on the game and even a film shown on the right.

From here on, many even stronger Chess Engines had appeared such as currently strongest open source chess engine Stockfish, Leela Chess Zero, Houdini, Komodo and so on. These engines are stronger than any human and are often used by GMs to analyse their game and possible lines.

The term Dynamisms refers to a playstyle where a player attempts to obtain an active position at the cost of material and long-term structure. Despite the short era where Dynamism has risen, most of these chess engines are materialistic, and prefers static games which is a style that is contrary to dynamic.

While this has transitioned most players to playing back to static style, another breakthrough in computers occured again, specifically in the field of artificial-intelligence.

The role of females in chess

While historically, females aren't widely restricted to the play in chess, they have a much lower ratio against males. This was very much true in the past and FIDE had encouraged many female players to play, opening up female only titles and tournaments.

This trend of having less female-to-male ratio persisted until today especially among the top players.

This can be indicated on the left visualisation where the difference is greatly shown by the logarithmic grouped bar chart.

However, in the modern era, the number of females players has been steadily increasing and can be shown by the dedicated female titles (starts with W).

While there are much less female players and especially less in the unrestricted titles, there was an exceptionally strong and talented female player.

Judit Polgár a Hungarian chess player born in 1976 had a reached a top rank of 8 in 2004. While there were other females who obtained the WGM title first, Polgár was the first to obtain the gender-unrestricted GM title at the age of 15 years and 4 months, at the time the youngest age record, beating the previous holder Bobby Fischer.

Additionally, she is the only woman to surpass 2700 Elo and the 10th place barrier; where she has beaten 11 current or former world champions and won several first place in tournaments.

While Polgár had retired in 2014, she had demonstrated a great example in the female community, not just in chess but in a competitive nature against odd gender ratios. Moreover, Polgár was also known to rarely play in women-specific tournaments and had said in an interview that "I always say that women should have the self-confidence that they are as good as male players, but only if they are willing to work and take it seriously as much as male players." -- Judit Polgár.

Chess in media

Can't talk about chess without mentioning Queen's Gambit. The 2020 Netflix series featuring Beth Harmon had attracted attention to many viewers even to non-chess players. The series had became Netflix's most watched miniseries and had been the top in a few countries.

The event had sparked many players to start playing chess and had increased the members joining in online websites and purchases of chess sets. The chess community including many GMs such as Carlsen, Polgár and Alexander Botez had reacted in positive reviews on its choreography and also the games.
